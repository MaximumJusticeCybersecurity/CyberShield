Opening line

Most teams are starting to rely on AI-generated recommendations, but they still need to know whether those recommendations are defensible. CyberShield takes an AI recommendation and turns it into an AI Trust Decision Record.

Demo path

  1. Open /vendor-risk.html.
  2. Enter first name, optional company, and optional vendor.
  3. Show the AI recommendation: approve the vendor because they have SOC 2, encrypt data, and appear low risk.
  4. Select a contradiction type.
  5. Point to claims, validators, evidence issues, and action tournament.
  6. Show why Request Evidence wins.
  7. Generate or print the AI Trust Decision Record.
  8. Explain capture as prototype-grade follow-up, not production CRM.

What to say at the AI recommendation

This sounds like the kind of recommendation a busy team might accept. It cites SOC 2, encryption, and low risk. CyberShield's job is to determine whether that conclusion is defensible.

What to say at validators

CyberShield does not score this with a fake trust number. It asks evidence questions: Is SOC 2 enough? Is encryption proven? Are subprocessors known? Is human approval required?

What to say at evidence issues

The system separates weak, missing, self-attested, contradictory, and scope-mismatched evidence. That matters because an AI recommendation can sound confident even when the record is not defensible.

What to say at action tournament

Approve loses. Accept with caveat is premature. Escalate is required but incomplete. Request Evidence wins because it preserves defensibility before approval.

Expected aha moment

The reviewer should realize CyberShield is not trying to be another chatbot. It creates a decision record that explains why the AI recommendation is or is not defensible.

Close

The first pilot is simple: give CyberShield three to five AI-generated recommendations your team might rely on. CyberShield produces Trust Decision Records showing claims, evidence gaps, risk if wrong, confidence band, human review, and the strongest defensible next action.

Objection handling

  • Is this AI judging AI? No. The prototype is deterministic and record-first. It checks claims, evidence, gaps, risk, and human review.
  • Is this production-ready? No. It is a static prototype and controlled pilot workflow.
  • Does this approve or reject vendors? No. It recommends the most defensible next action. In this sample, that action is Request Evidence.
  • Does SOC 2 mean approve? No. SOC 2 is evidence, not automatic approval.
  • Is framework mapping compliance proof? No. Mapping shows relevance. Compliance requires implementation and operating evidence.
