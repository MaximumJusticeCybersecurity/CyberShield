# AIDA Customer Discovery Guide

Status: Initial field guide  
Owner: Dr. Max Justice

## Objective

Validate the actual day-in-the-life workflow in which a person or organization receives an AI-generated recommendation and must decide whether to rely on it.

Do not ask only whether a participant likes CyberShield.  Ask participants to show how the decision is made today.

## Initial interview targets

- CISOs
- CIOs
- CFOs
- Chief Risk Officers
- Chief Compliance Officers
- Internal Audit leaders
- General Counsel or legal-risk leaders
- Federal program and mission leaders
- Federal contractors in high-assurance environments
- Healthcare security, privacy, clinical, and compliance leaders
- Financial-services security, risk, and operations leaders
- AI governance leaders
- GRC, assurance, cybersecurity, and managed-service providers

## Core questions

1. Tell me about the last time an AI system produced a recommendation that could materially affect the business, mission, customer, patient, employee, system, or regulator.
2. What happened from the moment the recommendation appeared until someone acted or declined to act?
3. Who owned the final decision?
4. What evidence did that person need?
5. What evidence was difficult or impossible to obtain?
6. How did the reviewer distinguish facts, assumptions, and AI-generated interpretation?
7. What caused escalation, delay, rejection, or approval?
8. What could have happened if the recommendation was wrong?
9. How reversible was the action?
10. What record had to be preserved for audit, regulation, litigation, customer assurance, or incident review?
11. How much time and labor did the review consume?
12. Where did people use spreadsheets, email, tickets, meetings, screenshots, or manual documents?
13. What would make an automated review credible enough to use?
14. What would make the organization reject an automated review?
15. Would this be purchased as software, an assessment, a managed service, or part of an existing advisory engagement?
16. Which executive owns the problem?
17. Which budget would pay?
18. What measurable outcome would justify purchase?
19. What current vendor, team, or process would CyberShield need to integrate with or displace?
20. Who else should we interview?

## Observation prompts

Ask the participant to show, where permitted and sanitized:

- a real approval flow;
- a vendor-risk review;
- a security or compliance recommendation;
- a ticket, report, or email chain;
- the evidence package;
- the approval or rejection record;
- the audit artifact; and
- the point where accountability becomes unclear.

Do not collect confidential, regulated, privileged, classified, export-controlled, or personally identifiable information in the repository.

## Buyer hypotheses to test

### Likely operational sponsors

- CISO
- Chief Risk Officer
- Chief Compliance Officer
- CIO

### Likely economic, risk, or approval stakeholders

- CFO
- General Counsel
- Internal Audit
- Data or AI governance leader
- Business or mission owner

These are hypotheses, not facts, until interviews validate them.

## Commercial models to test

1. AI Decision Assurance Review: fixed-fee review of one AI-influenced decision workflow.
2. Managed Decision Assurance: recurring review and monitoring of AI-generated recommendations.
3. Enterprise Platform License: integration into AI, GRC, ticketing, approval, or workflow systems.
4. Partner-enabled assurance: delivery through consulting, GRC, cybersecurity, audit, or managed-service providers.

## Interview evidence record

For every interview, capture:

- participant role and industry;
- date;
- problem observed;
- current workflow;
- decision owner;
- evidence required;
- time and cost indicators;
- risk if wrong;
- adoption barriers;
- buyer and budget hypothesis;
- exact language used by the participant;
- interest level;
- follow-up action; and
- whether the interview supports, weakens, or contradicts current assumptions.

## Validation threshold before major expansion

Before broadening the pilot, seek repeated evidence that:

- the workflow occurs frequently enough to matter;
- a named owner has accountability;
- the current process is costly, slow, inconsistent, or indefensible;
- a credible buyer and budget exist;
- CyberShield's output changes a decision or reduces material work; and
- the organization will provide a real pilot case or pay for a controlled assessment.
