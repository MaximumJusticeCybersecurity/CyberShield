# AIDA Manifesto v1.0

Owner: Dr. Max Justice  
Status: Owner-approved foundational doctrine  
Effective date: 2026-07-13

## Why AIDA exists

Organizations are deploying AI faster than they can confidently govern the recommendations and decisions AI produces on their behalf.

AIDA exists to help organizations determine whether an AI-generated recommendation is trustworthy enough to influence a human or autonomous decision.

## The problem

AI can generate polished recommendations faster than organizations can validate the claims, evidence, assumptions, consequences, and authority behind them.

The enterprise problem is not merely whether an AI model is capable.  The enterprise problem is whether a specific AI-generated recommendation is sufficiently supported, explainable, bounded, and safe enough to act upon.

## The output

AIDA produces a defensible AI Trust Decision Record.

The record shall show:

- what the AI recommended;
- what claims were made;
- what evidence supports or contradicts those claims;
- what evidence is missing;
- what assumptions and inferences were used;
- what happens if the recommendation is wrong;
- what confidence is justified and why;
- what human authority is required;
- what action is recommended; and
- what the final accountable human decided.

## Governing principle

> The methodology defines the product.  The product does not define the methodology.

## Core principles

1. AI recommendations are not facts.
2. Trust must be earned through evidence.
3. Evidence quality matters more than polished language.
4. Every recommendation carries consequence.
5. Risk if wrong must be considered before confidence or action.
6. Confidence must be explainable.
7. Missing and contradictory evidence must remain visible.
8. Human authority must be meaningful, not ceremonial.
9. Governance must occur before consequential execution.
10. Every consequential decision must be auditable.
11. No action, deferral, rejection, or a request for more evidence are legitimate outcomes.
12. AIDA must remain model-agnostic, vendor-neutral, and future-platform compatible.
13. AI confidence is not evidence.
14. Provenance is necessary but does not prove truth.
15. The system must distinguish AI claims, CyberShield analysis, and final human decisions.

## What AIDA is not

AIDA is not:

- a generic trust score;
- another dashboard that requires executives to interpret opaque metrics;
- a substitute for accountable human judgment;
- a claim that one AI can prove another AI correct;
- a compliance certification;
- a model vendor endorsement;
- an autonomous approval engine; or
- a reason to expand AI authority without evidence.

## North Star

AIDA succeeds when an accountable executive can state:

> I understand why this AI recommendation should or should not be trusted, what could happen if it is wrong, and what authority is required before action.

## Product boundary

CyberShield shall focus on the recommendation or decision artifact.

Its primary question is:

> Can the organization defend acting on this AI-generated recommendation based on the evidence available at the time?

System-wide model assurance, adversarial testing, deployment certification, and runtime controls may integrate with AIDA, but they shall not displace the current recommendation-level decision-assurance mission without explicit owner approval.

## Final rule

If a proposed feature does not improve an organization's ability to determine whether an AI-generated recommendation should be trusted before action is taken, it does not belong in the current product.
