## The Market Problem

Organizations are adopting AI faster than they are building defensible decision processes around AI-generated recommendations.

The risk is not only that AI might be wrong.

The sharper risk is that a confident AI-generated recommendation becomes enterprise action without a record showing:

- What was claimed
- What evidence supported it
- What evidence was missing
- What would happen if it was wrong
- Who reviewed it
- Why the organization acted

## Why Existing Categories Are Not Enough

### AI Governance Platforms

They often focus on policies, inventories, principles, approvals, and dashboards.

CyberShield focuses on a specific recommendation and whether it is defensible enough to act on.

### GRC Platforms

They manage controls, risks, issues, assessments, and evidence repositories.

CyberShield operates at the moment an AI-generated recommendation may influence action.

### Vendor-Risk Tools

They collect questionnaires, SOC reports, and vendor artifacts.

CyberShield asks whether an AI recommendation about a vendor is supported by the right evidence, scope, date, and review state.

### LLM Monitoring Tools

They observe model behavior, prompts, outputs, and runtime patterns.

CyberShield evaluates whether a business decision based on an AI output is defensible.

### Generic Trust Scores

They may compress uncertainty into a number.

CyberShield breaks the recommendation apart into claims, evidence, gaps, Risk If Wrong, confidence, human review, and defensible action.

### Manual Review

Manual review is necessary but inconsistent.

CyberShield structures the review so the record survives later scrutiny.

## Why CyberShield Is Different

CyberShield is built around the AI Trust Decision Record.

The record separates:

1. What the AI claimed
2. What the evidence says
3. What CyberShield inferred
4. What the human reviewer decided

That separation is the product advantage.
